Media Literacy and A Tale of Two Cities

Learning Objective:

  • Students will be able to identify and apply various persuasive techniques and logical fallacies
  • Students will create a product in which they apply analysis of literature alongside understanding of ways in which media can manipulate audiences

Pacing Guide Standards:

  • 9.1 The student will make planned oral presentations independently and in small groups
    • Advanced Academic Extension: The student will build expertise in oral communication by crafting presentations for authentic audiences, and making verbal and nonverbal choices appropriate to the audience and purpose
  • 9.2 The student will produce, analyze, and evaluate auditory, visual, and written media messages
    • Advanced Academic Extension: The student will analyze the ways in which media messages affect one’s own understanding of the world in order to understand the impact of the media in society

 

Background Preparation

In previous classes, students have:

Class 1:                 Learned media literacy terms through a  webquest and created an individual blog post demonstrating understanding of knowledge gained

Class 2:                 Watched youtube videos in class introducing logical fallacies

Discussed the benefit of different types of advertising.

Imagined themselves as revolutionaries in France trying to garner support for their cause as:

1.)    Fellow poor people in France

2.)    The middle class (Bourgeoisie)

3.)    The populace of neighboring countries (such as England)

Worked in small groups to generate ideas and shared with the class, then discussed the effectiveness of the created advertisements.

 

The Commercial Tie-in Project

1.       Introduce the project to students:

                                                   i.      Review the “Advertising Campaign” handout with students. Explain that students will work in self-selected groupings to select one product for further development from their earlier blog posts.  

                                                 ii.      Allow students to separate into groups and decide on product.

2.       Pass out planning guide to students. Tell students that these should be completed individually, for homework.

3.       The next class, have students return to their groups after checking that all planning guides are complete. Pass out storyboard planners to groups and have groups work together to thoroughly plan commercials before heading into the video editing lab.

4.       In the media lab, introduce students to Windows Movie Maker and/or Photo Story 3. (I gave students the option of creating a live-action commercial using Movie Maker or still-photo commercial with voiceover using Photo Story. You may want to limit your students’ choices depending on equipment available; we had limited working cameras at the time.) Demonstrate the main editing tools available in each program using Vision on students’ computers. Point out sites with public domain images and music available for the project, such as:

1.       Soundzabound.com (music)

2.       Publicdomainpictures.net (images)

Allow students time to record/create their commercial in the lab. This will take 2-3 days of editing time.

5.       Introduce students to the idea of a “pitch.” Remind them (as stated on the original directions sheet) that they will have to present their commercial to the class and convince them that their product is marketable.  Tell students they should spend this day researching their potential market to discover saturation, disposable income of target area, etc, to use in their pitch. Pass out the handout “The Pitch” to help students prepare.

6.       Select students to present their products to the advisory board (the class). Have the class select one product to push forward into development based on quality of commercial, presentation, and marketability. Discuss with the class which products they felt had the best chances of being sold and why, what techniques they saw being used, and how effectively the commercials played on logical fallacies.
