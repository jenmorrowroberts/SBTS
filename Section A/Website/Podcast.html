Comparing Nonfiction Texts Podcast

                             

Inspired by Malia Cameron’s Unit on Comparing Nonfiction Voices for 11th graders presented at the Summer 2011 English Inservice  

Podcasting Project: 

 

Objective of project:  This unit encourages students to look beyond their lives here in Fairfax County and learn more about a social issue in the world today while differing approaches to the same issue and analyzing their texts for potential flaws in logic and bias.

 

Target class:  English 9 Honors

 

This unit addresses portions of the standards for non-fiction reading. For 9th grade, the SOL components include:

 

9.1 The student will make planned oral presentations independently and in small groups

Advanced Academic Extension: The student will build expertise in oral communication by crafting

presentations for authentic audiences, and making verbal and nonverbal choices appropriate to the

audience and purpose (Practice)

 

9.4  The student will read, comprehend, and analyze a variety of literary texts including narratives, narrative nonfiction, poetry and drama

Advanced Academic Extension: The student will act as a literary critic by comparing elements of literature

within and between a variety of genres, and by analyzing the purposeful ways authors use language and

techniques to shape meaning and affect the audience (Practice/Connections)

 

9.5 The student will read and analyze a variety of nonfiction texts

 

Unit Overview:                                

Part I:  Students will work in partners (or groups of three) to read either a nonfiction “factual” book or a memoir on the same topic. By definition, the “factual” book would be written by a journalist or second-hand observer while the memoir would be written by someone who experienced a facet of the social issue firsthand. Students will choose titles that interest them. As an alternative to working with a partner, students may choose to work on their own and read an additional short personal essay or article from the alternative perspective for means of comparison. Because of the nature of some books students might choose, I had them fill out and return a permission slip from their parents.

 

Part II:   While reading, students should annotate passages from their books that are especially salient, meaningful, or informative. Each student should record numerous selections that help form his opinion.

 

Part III:  As a group (or at least in pairs), the students produce a Statement of Action about the social issue. The Statement of Action includes both a basis for the belief and a call to action that the students hope to enact either in the school or their community. If there is great dissent amid the group, the group could be split to allow students to voice opposing sides.

 

Part IV: Students plan and record podcasts as a means of presenting their findings on the issue.

 

A.      On the day you have assigned students to have their books finished, ask them to bring them to class, fully annotated.

B.      Begin class by asking if any students have listened to podcasts before and ask for examples. Then play a few minutes of different kinds of podcasts, asking students to pay attention to content and format. Radiolab and Freakanomics from NPR often have interesting topics (without inappropriate language) and are very different, stylistically.

C.      After playing a few minutes of the first podcast, ask students for their observations. Some things they might note: causal, yet professional dialogue, some use of sound effects or music, some have call-ins or interviews, some is just discussion between hosts, etc.

D.      Try to find a podcast with a different feel, stylistically, and play a few minutes then ask students to compare.

E.       After discussing the example podcasts, go over the handout, Podcast Instructions along with the rubric.

F.       After going over any questions students have, pass out the graphic organizer and have students use this to prepare before going to the lab so that time isn’t wasted.

G.     In the computer lab, if this is the first time using Audacity, use Vision to demonstrate to students how to record and manipulate their audio. Students will need 2-3 days in the lab to record, edit, and polish their podcasts.

H.      When they are finished, have students export their final podcasts as mp3 files. You can then set up a podcast feed in Blackboard so that students can hear each other’s podcasts. You can create an assignment in Blackboard for students to submit their work and then upload their mp3. If you want students to be able to add their own podcast to the feed, unfortunately, they have to record directly into Blackboard, which means they cannot edit their recordings and I don’t recommend if you plan to grade them on public speaking and the quality of their product.
